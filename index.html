Charles Pletcher / Columbia University
SCS 2019

Antígona furiosa toma el tema de Antígona, entresaca textos de la obra original y de otras obras, arma una nueva Antígona fuera del tiempo para que paradójicamente nos cuente su historia en su tiempo y en el nuestro. (Contreras, 143 n. 19)


Antígona furiosa takes Antigone's theme, culls texts from the original and from other works, arms a new Antigone outside of time so that, paradoxically, she tells us her story in her time and in ours.

Antígona ahorcada. Ciñe sus cabellos una corona de flores blancas, marchitas. Después de un momento, lentamente, afloja y quita el lazo de su cuello, se acomoda el vestido blanco y sucio. Se mueve, canturreando. (Gambaro, 197)


Antígona hanged. A wreath of white flowers, wilted, girds her hair. After a moment, slowly, she loosens and removes the noose around her neck; she adjusts her dirty white dress. She moves, singing softly.

Hamlet 4.5.34-37

Les corps ne sont pas du «plein», de l’espace remplie (l’espace est partout remplie): ils sont l’espace ouvert, c’est-à-dire en un sens l’espace proprement spacieux plutôt que spatial, ou ce qu’on peut encore nommer le lieu. Les corps sont des lieux d’existence, et il n’y a pas d’existence sans lieu, sans là, sans un «ici», «voici», pour le ceci. (Nancy, 15)


Bodies aren’t some “fullness” or filled space (space is filled everywhere): they are open space, which is to say in a sense properly spacious space rather than spatial space, or what could also be called place. Bodies are places of existence, and there is no existence without place, without there, without a “here,” “here is,” for this.

[W]hereas most messengers remain featureless ciphers, serving merely to channel news to others, this Guard is one of the more colourful characters in Greek tragedy: a garrulous, cowardly, yet witty figure whose selfish preoccupations and practical perspective throw into relief the more high-minded ideals of the main characters. La batalla. Irrumpe entrechocar metálico de espadas, piafar de caballos, gritos y ayes imprecisos. Antígona se aparta. Mira desde el palacio. Cae al suelo, golpean sus piernas, de un lado y de otro, con un ritmo que se acrecienta al paroxismo, como si padeciera la batalla en carne propia. (Gambaro, 199)


The battle. The metallic clash of swords erupts, the hoof-beating of horses, indeterminate cries and moans. Antígona draws apart. She looks away from the palace. She falls to the ground, her legs kick, from one side to the other, with a rhythm that grows to paroxysm, as if she were suffering the battle in her own flesh.
